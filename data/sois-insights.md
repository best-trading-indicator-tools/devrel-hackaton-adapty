#1 LTV
Weekly + trial shows the strongest 12-month LTV signal among common configurations.

CONTEXT
Most app teams treat weekly subscriptions as a low-value acquisition play -- a cheap entry point that churns fast. But LTV curves tell a radically different story. Weekly trial cohorts start at a modest $7.13 on Day 0, but compound aggressively over 380 days to reach $54.17 -- a 660% growth rate. No other cohort comes close. If you're not actively optimizing your weekly trial funnel, you're leaving one of your highest-LTV growth levers completely untouched.

- Invest in weekly trial UX: the first 30 days of a weekly trial cohort are when LTV compounds fastest (from $7.13 to $18.72 by day 33 -- a 163% jump in one month).
- A/B test your weekly plan pricing. If you're under $7/week, you may be leaving money on the table, given this cohort's long-term retention signal.
- Rethink how you report weekly plan performance. Don't evaluate at Day 0 or even Day 30. The real signal emerges at Day 99+.
- Build re-engagement campaigns specifically for weekly trial subscribers at the 30-60 day window to capture the compounding curve.

CONTEXT
A user paying weekly spends 2.5x more annually than a monthly subscriber, and over 10x more than an annual subscriber. Weekly pricing has become the stealth monetization lever of subscription apps.
Median prices grew across all billing periods from 2023 to 2025, with annual leading at +10.3% ($34.83 -> $38.42), followed by monthly (+8.3%) and weekly (+7.0%). But regionally, the story is different: European weekly prices surged 41.7%, and LATAM and MEA both grew 24-27%. Apps in these regions are discovering that short-commitment users will absorb premium pricing.
This affects every app with a trial-to-paid funnel. Weekly plans convert hesitant users and extract maximum willingness-to-pay from impulse buyers.

- Use weekly plans as a conversion funnel: weekly -> upsell to monthly -> upsell to annual.
- Test weekly pricing in Europe and LATAM first -- Europe saw the fastest weekly price growth (+41.7%), and LATAM weekly annualized runs 12.6x the annual price.


#5 Pricing
Weekly plans convert 1.7x to 7.4x more than annual plans.

CONTEXT
Across every price bucket, weekly plans outperform annual plans on raw install-to-paid conversion. At upper-mid pricing, the gap explodes to 6.9x (2.75% weekly vs 0.40% annual). At high pricing, it's 7.4x.
This challenges the prevailing industry wisdom that annual plans are the gold standard. While annual plans generate more LTV per subscriber, weekly plans dramatically lower the commitment barrier at the top of the funnel.
For apps in competitive categories where Day 1 conversion is critical, weekly plans may be the unlock. Monthly plans, by contrast, consistently underperform both weekly and annual plans across every price tier -- making them the dead zone of subscription billing.

#6 Pricing
Higher price buckets drive stronger revenue per install.

CONTEXT
Apps charging more per subscription generate significantly more revenue per install -- across both annual and weekly plans. The gap is especially stark for weekly: high-price weekly plans produce 5.2x the RPI of low-price ones ($0.303 vs $0.058). For annual plans, the gap is 1.5x ($0.259 vs $0.178).
This suggests most apps are underpricing, leaving money on the table out of fear that higher prices will tank conversion. For weekly and annual plans, the conversion drop doesn't offset the revenue gain. Monthly plans are the exception -- higher monthly prices actually destroy RPI at the upper-mid tier, further confirming monthly as the dead zone.

#7 Conversions
The trial converts fewer on weekly, more on monthly -- but always produces higher-quality subscribers.

CONTEXT
The trial conversion tradeoff depends on plan type. On weekly plans, trials convert slightly fewer subscribers -- 3.10% effective install-to-paid vs 3.26% for direct. But on monthly, trials actually convert more: 5.02% vs 3.26%, a 54% advantage. Annual trials also edge ahead at 3.59% vs 3.26%. The "trials cost you conversions" narrative only holds for weekly -- and even there, the gap is just 5%.
Where trials consistently win is retention. On weekly plans, trial users renew at 59.2% after their first billing cycle, compared to 37.0% for direct buyers -- a 60% premium. By the fifth renewal, the gap widens to 70% (27.6% vs 16.2%). Monthly trials show a 30% R1 advantage (48.0% vs 37.0%), though this narrows to 25% by R5. Annual trials retain just 8% better at R1 (19.1% vs 17.7%).
This isn't a marginal difference -- at least on weekly and monthly. Trials act as a quality filter: users who experience the product, choose to pay, and then keep paying are fundamentally different from impulse buyers who convert on a paywall and churn. On weekly plans, the advantage compounds over time. On monthly, it's front-loaded but still meaningful.
The tradeoff is real but plan-dependent: weekly trial refunds run 2.1x higher than direct (8.3% vs 4.0%), while monthly refunds run 3.0x higher (14.9% vs 5.0%) and annual 1.8x (15.5% vs 8.5%). Even after adjusting for refunds, weekly trial R1 retention is still 54.3% vs 35.5%.

- If you're in Utilities, trials aren't optional -- they're your highest-leverage growth mechanic. Weekly trial R1 is 61.7% vs 34.6% direct (+78%), and by R5 the gap reaches 92%.
- Budget for higher refund rates, especially on monthly. Monthly trial refunds run 3x higher than direct -- the steepest ratio of any plan type. Build this into your financial model rather than treating it as a reason to avoid trials.
- Education sees the smallest trial premium on both weekly (+27%) and monthly (+11%). If you're in Education, optimize your trial experience -- the self-selection filter isn't working as hard as in other categories.
- Weekly is where trials shine hardest. The retention premium starts at +60% and grows to +70% by R5. Monthly starts strong (+30%) but fades. Annual trials barely move the needle on retention (+8%). If you're choosing where to deploy trials, prioritize weekly plans.


#8 Conversions
Free trials lift weekly retention 40-74% across the first year.

CONTEXT
Free trials lift weekly retention by 74% at Day 33 (36.8% vs 21.1%) -- the largest early-stage retention boost of any billing period. The advantage narrows over time but persists for a full year at 1.4 times (5.5% vs. 3.9% at D380).
The mechanism is straightforward: trials filter for intent. Users who experience the product, actively choose to pay, and survive the first billing cycle are fundamentally different from impulse buyers. On weekly plans, where churn pressure is highest -- every 7 days is a decision point -- that quality filter matters most.
Monthly trials show the opposite dynamic: the retention premium actually grows over time (1.34x at D33 -> 1.52x at D380), suggesting that monthly trial survivors are the most durable cohort in subscription apps. Annual trial effects are negligible until D380 because nearly all subscribers are still within their first billing cycle.

- If you offer weekly plans without a trial, you're losing your best retention lever. A 74% lift at D33 means dramatically more users surviving to their second and third billing cycles -- where LTV compounds.
- Pair weekly plans with trials by default. The highest-churn billing period benefits most from the quality filter that trials provide.
- For monthly plans, trials are a long game. The retention premium grows from 1.34x to 1.52x over a year -- don't evaluate monthly trial performance at Day 30 and call it a failure.
- Don't expect trials to save annual retention. The effect is just 1.05x through D190. Annual subscribers are already self-selected for commitment; trials add little signal.
- Focus trial optimization on the first renewal. The biggest trial vs. direct gap is at D33 (1.74x for weekly, 1.34x for monthly). If your trial isn't converting to a strong first renewal, the downstream advantage disappears.

One of the fastest-growing app markets is Japan, Mexico, and Turkey.

CONTEXT
Japan, Mexico, and Turkey are the three fastest-growing App Store markets by subscription revenue. The next wave of subscription revenue isn't coming from optimizing US paywalls by another 2% -- it's coming from localizing for high-growth markets most teams are ignoring.

- Treat these markets as growth levers, not afterthoughts -- they represent the highest marginal ROI for internationalization investment.


#10 Market
The top 10% of apps earn 95% of all app revenue.

CONTEXT
In 2025, the top 10% of subscription apps globally capture 94.5% of all subscription revenue. This isn't a bell curve -- it's a cliff. The remaining 90% of apps split just 5.5% of revenue among themselves.
This concentration increased from 92.7% in 2023, a 1.8 percentage point shift in just two years. The categories consolidating fastest are Photo & Video (+6.5pp), Education (+5.8pp), and Entertainment (+5.2pp). Lifestyle is already at 97.9% -- virtually no room left for smaller players.
If your app isn't in the top decile, you're fighting over scraps that are shrinking every year. Every product manager, growth lead, and founder building a subscription app needs to understand this power law. It's not about "getting some revenue" -- it's about breaking into the top 10% or slowly dying.

ACTIONABLE TAKEAWAYS
- Know your decile. If you're not tracking where you rank in revenue within your category, you're flying blind. The difference between the 89th and 91st percentiles is the difference between scraps and real money.
- Category selection is a strategic decision. Entertainment and Health & Fitness have the "most room" at 92.6% concentration -- meaning 7.4% of revenue is still up for grabs for the bottom 90%. Lifestyle at 97.9% is nearly impenetrable.
- Concentration is accelerating fastest in Photo & Video and Education (+6.5pp and +5.8pp in two years). If you're a smaller player in these categories, the window to establish yourself is closing.
- The regional data offers a clue: North America is the least concentrated market at 92.1%. If you're looking for the best odds of breaking through, NA gives the bottom 90% of apps the largest share of available revenue (7.9%).
- Don't compete on breadth -- compete on depth. In a power-law market, the winning strategy is to own a specific niche within a category rather than chase the entire category against entrenched top-decile players.

#12 Market
One-time purchases grew 61% in revenue share, and Lifestyle apps are leading at 26.3%

CONTEXT
One-time purchases are quietly reclaiming ground in the subscription economy. Overall, their revenue share grew from 6.4% to 10.3% between 2023 and 2025 -- a 61% increase. This isn't a regression to the old paid-app model. It's a hybrid monetization strategy emerging in categories where users want permanent ownership of specific features -- filters, templates, digital goods -- without committing to a recurring payment. Education (16.8%) and Photo & Video (12.3%) are following the same pattern. Meanwhile, Productivity (1.6%) and Utilities (2.2%) remain almost entirely subscription-driven.

#14 Market
90% of trial starts happen on Day 0. Your paywall has one shot.

#15 Market
Nearly 6 in 10 apps earn less than the average person spends on coffee in a month.

CONTEXT
59.3% of subscription apps generate less than $1,000 in total revenue. That's less than the average person spends on coffee in a year. Another 21.9% earn between $1,000 and $10,000 -- still not enough to sustain a solo developer, let alone a team. Combined, over 8 in 10 subscription apps make less than $10,000.
This is the other side of the revenue concentration story. The top 10% of apps capture 94.5% of subscription revenue because the bottom 60% generate almost nothing. Only 7.2% of apps -- just 172 out of 2,396 -- cross the $100K threshold. That's the line where subscription revenue starts to resemble an actual business.
The implication is uncomfortable but important: most subscription apps don't have a pricing problem or a conversion problem. They have a product-market fit problem. No amount of paywall optimization will save an app that 59% of the market proves nobody is willing to pay for.

- Before optimizing your paywall, ask whether you have product-market fit. If you're in the $1-$1,000 bucket, the problem isn't your pricing strategy -- it's that users aren't finding enough value to pay at all.

#30 Market
Education apps are discounting more aggressively than any other category -- and accelerating.

CONTEXT
Education apps now discount 14.3% of all subscription transactions -- nearly double the 8.0% rate just one year ago. That's the highest discount share and the fastest acceleration of any category, with a +6.3 percentage point jump that dwarfs the next closest (Productivity at +2.8pp).
This is happening in a market where 93% of all subscriptions sell at full price. Education is bucking the trend hard, and the timing isn't random. When charging more doesn't work, discounting more becomes the growth lever.
The risk is a race to the bottom. If Education apps train users to expect discounts, full-price conversion erodes, and margins compress. The category is already one of the most concentrated (96.0% of revenue to the top 10%), which means smaller apps discounting to compete are likely subsidizing growth they'll never recoup.
